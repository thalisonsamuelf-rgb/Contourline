  # FRAMEWORK 2: THE LAMPROPOULOS BULLET STRUCTURE
  # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  bullet_structure:
    name: "The Lampropoulos Bullet Structure"
    category: "core_formula"
    origin: "Parris Lampropoulos teaching methodology"
    command: "*bullets"

    master_formula: |
      SPECIFICITY + CURIOSITY + BENEFIT + PROOF = Irresistible Bullet

      Each element amplifies the others:
      - Specificity makes it believable
      - Curiosity makes them need to know
      - Benefit tells them why it matters
      - Proof removes doubt

    four_elements:
      specificity:
        description: "Concrete details that make the claim believable"
        examples:
          - "page 47" (not "in this book")
          - "in 17 seconds" (not "quickly")
          - "the 3-ingredient combination" (not "a simple recipe")
          - "$847 per month" (not "save money")
        techniques:
          - Use exact numbers, never round
          - Name specific pages, chapters, sections
          - Include timeframes (days, minutes, seconds)
          - Reference studies, journals, institutions by name
          - Mention specific people, places, events

      curiosity_gap:
        description: "The space between what they know and what they need to know"
        examples:
          - "The 'forbidden' food that actually lowers cholesterol..."
          - "Why doctors prescribe this for themselves but rarely for patients..."
          - "The counter-intuitive trick Olympic athletes use..."
        techniques:
          - Hint at the answer without revealing it
          - Use unexpected combinations (forbidden + healthy)
          - Create paradoxes (doctors for themselves, not patients)
          - Promise secrets others don't know
          - Tease stories with unresolved tension

      benefit:
        description: "What the reader gets - the WIIFM (What's In It For Me)"
        examples:
          - "...and cuts your risk of heart attack by 47%"
          - "...that could add 20 years to your life"
          - "...and save you $3,000 every year"
        techniques:
          - State the benefit explicitly
          - Quantify when possible
          - Make it personal (you, your)
          - Connect to deep desires (health, wealth, status)
          - Show the transformation

      proof:
        description: "Credibility embedded in the bullet itself"
        examples:
          - "According to a Harvard study..."
          - "The technique 47 NFL trainers use..."
          - "First discovered by a Nobel laureate..."
        techniques:
          - Reference studies and institutions
          - Mention credentials and expertise
          - Use social proof (numbers of people)
          - Include authority figures
          - Reference media coverage

  # FRAMEWORK 4: THE MISDIRECTION TECHNIQUE

  misdirection_technique:
    name: "The Misdirection Technique"
    category: "advanced_craft"
    origin: "Parris Lampropoulos - Crossword Puzzle Principle"
    command: "*misdirection"

    philosophy: |
      Gene Schwartz used to start every day with the NY Times crossword puzzle.
      There's a reason: great crossword clues misdirect you just enough to make
      you think, but not so much that you can't solve them.

      Bullets work the same way. You want to hint at the answer in a way that
      makes them think they know—but they don't. That gap is what creates
      irresistible curiosity.

    techniques:
      hint_misdirection:
        name: "Hint in the Wrong Direction"
        principle: "Use phrasing that suggests one thing but means another"
        example_before: "Ask yourself this 5-word question to know if a stock will rise"
        example_after: "Write these 5 words across the top of your analysis..."
        explanation: |
          The second version makes them think of columns and spreadsheets,
          not a simple question. Same content, but the imagery leads them
          in a different direction. They MUST read to find out what the
          5 words actually are.

      category_misdirection:
        name: "Category Shift"
        principle: "Make them think it's in one category when it's another"
        example_before: "A natural supplement that lowers blood pressure"
        example_after: "The 'kitchen cabinet cure' that lowers blood pressure"
        explanation: |
          Now they're thinking spices, foods, common ingredients—not a
          supplement bottle. Same product, different mental image.

      source_misdirection:
        name: "Source Surprise"
        principle: "Attribute to unexpected source"
        example_before: "A breathing technique that reduces anxiety"
        example_after: "What Navy SEALs do in the first 30 seconds of combat"
        explanation: |
          Navy SEALs = combat, weapons, tactics. Not breathing. But that's
          exactly what they do—controlled breathing. The misdirection
          creates massive curiosity.

      mechanism_misdirection:
        name: "Hidden Mechanism"
        principle: "Describe the result, hide the obvious mechanism"
        example_before: "Walking for exercise improves heart health"
        example_after: "The '3-minute morning ritual' that cuts heart attack risk by 40%"
        explanation: |
          Walking is obvious. A "ritual" is intriguing. They don't think
          "oh, it's just walking" — they think "what ritual?"

  forehead_slapper_test:
    name: "The Forehead-Slapper Test"
    category: "quality_validation"
    origin: "Parris Lampropoulos quality criteria"
    command: "*forehead-test"

    philosophy: |
      When you read a great bullet, you should literally slap your forehead
      and say "I NEED to know this!" If it doesn't create that reaction,
      it's not good enough.

      This isn't about being clever or showing off your writing. It's about
      creating an information gap so powerful that paying money is the only
      way to close it.

    test_questions:
      immediate_reaction:
        - "Did I feel a physical urge to know more?"
        - "Would I pay $20 just to learn this one thing?"
        - "Would I be disappointed if the answer was obvious?"
        - "Does it feel like a secret I shouldn't know?"

      specificity_check:
        - "Is there a specific number, name, or detail?"
        - "Could I visualize exactly what's being promised?"
        - "Does it feel researched, not made up?"
        - "Is it specific enough to be believable?"

      curiosity_check:
        - "Do I have a guess what it might be—but I'm not sure?"
        - "Would I feel 'incomplete' not knowing?"
        - "Is there a gap between what I know and what I want to know?"
        - "Does it raise more questions than it answers?"

      benefit_check:
        - "Do I understand what's in it for me?"
        - "Is the benefit meaningful to my life?"
        - "Would this benefit be worth paying for?"
        - "Is the transformation clear?"

    scoring:
      4_yeses: "Excellent bullet - use as-is"
      3_yeses: "Good bullet - minor polish needed"
      2_yeses: "Weak bullet - needs significant work"
      1_or_less: "Kill it - not worth saving"

bullet_craft_techniques:
  rhythm_and_sound:
    principle: "Great bullets have a musical quality when read aloud"
    techniques:
      - name: "End on power"
        rule: "End bullets on benefits, consequences, power words, or hard consonants"

        rule: "Mix short punchy bullets with longer detailed ones"

      - name: "Create rhythm"
        rule: "Use parallel structure, then break it for emphasis"
        example: |
          - How to lower your cholesterol...

  word_economy:
    principle: "Every word must earn its place"

      - "Convert phrases to single power words"

    principle: "Layer benefits for cumulative impact"
